Women in
College Sports

Over the past decade, women’s collegiate sports have seen growth in participation and revenue, yet the playing field remains far from equal. Using NCAA-reported data from 2012 to 2022, this interactive narrative explores the evolving landscape of women’s sports, uncovering persistent gender disparities in financial aid, operating expenses, and representation. How has the female sports landscape changed from 2012 to 2022?

Women's sports popularity ranking based on participation over the years for NCAA schools. Annual revenue is encoded by the area of circle.

The general landscape of women’s sports has remained fairly consistent over the last 10 years, with soccer being the most popular sport for women to participate in, while basketball has consistently had the highest annual revenue.

 
 
 

Revenue generated by DI FBS constitutes a disproportionately larger share of total revenue compared to its share of female athlete participation.

For the remainder of our narrative we will focus only on schools classified as D1 A/FBS, as it makes up the biggest portion of athletes and generated revenue for women. Basketball consistently generates the most average revenue per participant for both men and women.

Basketball and volleyball show the greatest increase in revenue per female participant over the years, while other sports any increase in women’s revenue per participant is minimal. Notably the ratio of women’s to men’s team revenue per participant improves significantly, shifting from 1:5 to 1:4, highlighting substantial progress compared to other sports.

 
 
 

Zooming in on the other sports...

Volleyball is the only sport where women’s revenue per participant consistently and significantly exceeds men’s revenue per participant. Women’s volleyball revenue per participant increased from $52,953 in 2012 to $99,526 in 2022. Tennis, golf, and softball follow volleyball, making these four sports join basketball in the top in revenue per participant for women’s teams.

 
 
 

Admission rate does not have a strong relationship with athlete participation for men or women.

Men and women’s participation does not seem to depend on admission rate. Further, there is no significant distinction between public and private universities, though private universities tend to cluster towards lower admission rates.

Where does Title IX come into play?

Title IX is a federal law, signed in 1972, that requires educational institutions receiving federal funding to provide equal opportunities to female students, including in sports. If a school is not compliant with Title IX it can theoretically lose federal funding, but in its history no school has lost federal funding despite non-compliance. Title IX states that universities must provide female athletes with comparable benefits and services to male athletes, such as equal locker rooms, facilities for training and competition, and coaching and tutoring, etc. A university must also meet the participation requirement, which states that participation slots for female and male athletes should be proportionate to the student body composition. This constraint, however, applies only at the individual level, not at the team level. If 50% of a student body is female, the school should have 50% of the individual athlete participation slots made for female athletes, but 50% of a school’s teams don’t have to be female. The main purpose of Title IX is to ensure equal access to financial aid. Funds allocated to athletic scholarships must be proportional to male and female athlete participation rates. For example, if a school has $500,000 in athletic scholarships and 45% of its athletes are female, it must allocate $225,000 to female athletes.

NCAA financial aid allocations have increased similarly for men and women

This view confirms that male and female participation rates have stayed fairly consistent, at around 58% and 42% respectively. Due to the constraints placed by Title IX we would expect the participation proportion for each gender to be equal to the proportion of financial aid received, but we see that in schools classified as D1 FBS, where the most female athletes participate and generate the most revenue, that men seem to receive more financial aid than they should based on participation rates, with men receiving on average across all NCAA schools closer to 56% of the financial aid allotment (when it should be closer to 54%).

 
 
 
 
 
 

Top 5 Most and Least Equitable Schools for Women’s Financial Aid Disbursement

Title IX requires that financial aid be distributed equitably, proportional to the participation rates of men and women in athletics. The top 5 most equitable schools show strong alignment between women’s athletic participation and the aid they receive. The 5 least equitable schools show significant discrepancies, revealing schools where female athletes may not be receiving their fair share of financial aid.

What about other expenses?

Title IX dictates financial disbursement, but it doesn’t regulate other spending. Operating expenses, also known as gameday expenses, includes all costs incurred from home, away, or neutral site contests, such as housing, food, equipment, and uniforms for all team members, coaches, and support staff.

Basketball is the most expensive sport, but also generates the most revenue for men and women

For the top 5 highest revenue generating women’s sports, this scatter plot shows the relationship between average operating expenses and average revenue. Men’s and women’s basketball both have higher average operating expenses than any other sport, but they also have the largest difference in operating expenses by gender.

 
 
 

Women’s basketball generate less revenue per operating dollar than men’s basketball

While all men’s basketball teams make approximately 3 times more money than they spend to operate the team, 46 women’s teams fall below this threshold, with 21 of those teams not even breaking even with their operating expenses (shown in the grey area).

The evolving landscape of women’s collegiate sports reveals persistent inequities compared to men’s sports. Despite some growth in popularity, increased participation, and progress in revenue generation, challenges remain in achieving equity in financial aid, operating expenses, and overall representation.
