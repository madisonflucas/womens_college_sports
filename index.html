Women in College Sports

Introduction

Women’s sports have grown in popularity in recent years, with more enhanced media coverage and excitement over specific athletes, but men’s sports are still overwhelmingly the face of college sports.

Through this website, we hope to guide you through the recent state of women’s sports and certain discrepancies that exist between men’s and women’s college sports.

Where does Title IX come into play?

Title IX is a federal law, signed in 1972, that requires educational institutions receiving federal funding to provide equal opportunities to female students, including in sports. If a school is not compliant with Title IX it can theoretically lose federal funding, but in its history no school has lost federal funding despite non-compliance. Title IX states that universities must provide female athletes with comparable benefits and services to male athletes, such as equal locker rooms, facilities for training and competition, and coaching and tutoring, etc. A university must also meet the participation requirement, which states that participation slots for female and male athletes should be proportionate to the student body composition. This constraint, however, applies only at the individual level, not at the team level. If 50% of a student body is female, the school should have 50% of the individual athlete participation slots made for female athletes, but 50% of a school’s teams don’t have to be female. The main purpose of Title IX is to ensure equal access to financial aid. Funds allocated to athletic scholarships must be proportional to male and female athlete participation rates For example, if a school has $500,000 in athletic scholarships and 45% of its athletes are female, it must allocate $225,000 to female athletes.

Title IX Aid vs Participation

Displayed are the trends in financial aid disbursement to male and female athletes, averaged for all NCAA schools each year. Most notably, we can see that NCAA allocations for financial aid have increased over the past 10 years, and that these allocations have increased seemingly proportionately for males and females. The bottom view confirms that male and female participation rates have stayed fairly consistent, at around 58% and 42% respectively. Due to the constraints placed by Title IX we would expect the participation proportion for each gender to be equal to the proportion of financial aid received, but we see that in schools classified as D1 FBS, where the most female athletes participate and generate the most revenue, that men seem to receive more financial aid than they should based on participation rates, with men receiving on average across all NCAA schools closer to 56% of the financial aid allotment (when it should be closer to 54%).
